# *Start Something That Matters* — Full Report

**Author:** Blake Mycoskie | **Published:** 2011 | **Publisher:** Spiegel & Grau (Random House) | **Genre:** Memoir / Business / Entrepreneurship

*Start Something That Matters* is a New York Times bestseller that combines Mycoskie's personal memoir of founding TOMS Shoes with a practical business guide. Its core message is that profit, passion, and purpose are not mutually exclusive — you can build a successful business and change the world at the same time. [goodreads](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/10723236-start-something-that-matters)

## The TOMS Origin Story

In 2006, Mycoskie took a vacation to Argentina, where he rediscovered the *alpargata* — a simple canvas shoe popular across the country. Traveling through villages, he met an American woman running a shoe drive and discovered that many children lacked footwear, leaving them vulnerable to blisters, infections, and disease. He initially considered starting a charity but concluded that a for-profit model would generate a more reliable, sustainable flow of shoes than unpredictable donations . The result was TOMS (short for "Tomorrow's Shoes"), built around the revolutionary "One for One" model: for every pair sold, one pair is given to a child in need. [supersummary](https://www.supersummary.com/start-something-that-matters/summary/)

He recruited his Argentine polo teacher Alejo as a partner, found a shoemaker, and produced 250 sample pairs. After pitching the story to a buyer and landing a *Los Angeles Times* fashion article, he received over 2,200 orders in a single day — with only about 160 pairs in stock. He hired interns via Craigslist, manufactured 4,000 more pairs in Argentina, and that first summer sold 10,000 pairs out of his Venice Beach apartment. [supersummary](https://www.supersummary.com/start-something-that-matters/summary/)

## The Six Keys: Core Framework of the Book

The book's backbone is six principles Mycoskie identifies as essential to building any meaningful venture. [books.google](https://books.google.com/books/about/Start_Something_That_Matters.html?id=PAgkmupiS3MC)

### Key 1: Find Your Story
A compelling narrative is more powerful than facts or advertising. Mycoskie opens this section with the founders of **method** (eco-friendly cleaning products), who built a $100M brand around their personal story of discovering harmful chemicals in household cleaners. He cites a Carnegie Mellon study showing that people donated more than twice as much after reading a personal story about a poor girl versus receiving cold statistics. His pivotal moment came at JFK Airport, where a stranger wearing TOMS passionately recounted the brand's entire story before Mycoskie revealed he was the founder — proof of the exponential power of story-driven customers. He distinguishes between mere "buyers" and "supporters" who buy into a mission, and advises readers to identify their passions, use community affiliations, and get their story in front of influential connectors. [shortform](https://www.shortform.com/summary/start-something-that-matters-summary-blake-mycoskie)

### Key 2: Face Your Fears
Fear is natural but it cannot be allowed to go unchecked . Mycoskie shares the story of his mother, **Pam Mycoskie**, a Texas stay-at-home mom who self-published *Butter Busters* despite being deeply introverted and having no publishing connections — the book eventually sold 1.4 million copies. He explains that fear is a fight-or-flight response that can't be controlled, but your *actions* in response to it can be. His personal "fear killers" include: writing fears down on paper to externalize them, thinking in small incremental steps (referencing the Japanese concept of *kaizen*), surrounding himself with enthusiastic team members, and reading entrepreneur biographies. He also recounts turning down a lucrative job offer from mentor Carl Westcott (founder of 1-800-FLOWERS) to pursue TOMS despite the financial risk. [supersummary](https://www.supersummary.com/start-something-that-matters/summary/)

### Key 3: Be Resourceful Without Resources
Starting with almost nothing is not a disadvantage — it forces creative problem-solving that becomes embedded in a company's culture. TOMS' early operations ran from Mycoskie's Venice Beach apartment with two unpaid interns recruited via Craigslist . He shares the story of a Nordstrom buyer who called demanding to speak to the sales department, then customer service, only to discover both were the founder and two interns in the same room — Nordstrom waited and became one of TOMS' largest customers. He cites Apple's garage, Facebook's dorm room, and Amazon's slow-build approach as examples of the power of resource constraints, contrasting them with dot-com-era failures like Pets.com and Webvan that raised hundreds of millions and collapsed. Practical tips include using free social media, leveraging free digital tools, and using unconventional job titles to obscure a small company's size. [supersummary](https://www.supersummary.com/start-something-that-matters/summary/)

### Key 4: Keep It Simple
Simplicity should be applied to every aspect of a business . Mycoskie profiles **Michele Sipolt Kapustka**, who turned a habit of mailing rubber balls with Sharpie messages into SENDaBALL — shipping over 20,000 balls by resisting all pressure to expand beyond its core concept. He uses Apple's iPod, Google's minimalist home page, Chipotle's streamlined menu, and Southwest Airlines' route conceived on a cocktail napkin as corporate examples of design simplicity. TOMS' own shoe design, the century-old *alpargata*, served as a blank canvas for collaborations precisely because it was so simple. Mycoskie also connects professional simplicity to personal simplicity, describing how he sold most of his possessions and moved onto a 200-square-foot sailboat after observing on Shoe Drops that the people with the least often seemed the happiest. [supersummary](https://www.supersummary.com/start-something-that-matters/summary/)

### Key 5: Build Trust
Trust is the foundation of lasting customer loyalty and a healthy company culture. Mycoskie profiles **Tony Hsieh**, CEO of Zappos, who grew the company to over $1 billion in annual sales before its $1.2 billion acquisition by Amazon, largely by treating employees as friends, allowing uncensored tweeting, and treating vendors as partners. He traces a historical arc from Frederick Taylor's 20th-century model of rigid worker supervision to the modern trust-based workplace, introducing the concept of "servant leadership" coined by Robert K. Greenleaf in 1970. He also profiles **Scott Harrison**, founder of **charity: water**, a former nightclub promoter who built a nonprofit using a "100 percent model" where every public dollar goes directly to water projects with GPS-verified results — in five years raising $22 million and providing clean water to 1 million people across 17 countries. Mycoskie admits his own trust-building mistakes, including creating 800 Airstream-themed shoes for a retiree convention that preferred orthopedic footwear. [supersummary](https://www.supersummary.com/start-something-that-matters/summary/)

### Key 6: Giving Is Good Business
Incorporating giving into a business model doesn't hurt the bottom line — it amplifies it . Mycoskie profiles **Lauren Bush**, co-founder of **FEED Projects**, who sells eco-friendly bags to fund school lunches, providing over 60 million meals at the time of writing. He challenges Milton Friedman's assertion that a company's only responsibility is profit, citing a Stanford study showing 97% of business students would forgo financial benefits to work for a socially responsible company. He surveys corporate giving at Timberland and Home Depot, and recounts how employees at Canadian bank CIBC organically grew a breast cancer fundraiser into a strategic initiative that raised a record $33 million in one year. Giving turns customers into active marketing partners — TOMS supporters organized "Operation TOMS Prom" across 35 high schools entirely on their own. [supersummary](https://www.supersummary.com/start-something-that-matters/summary/)

## Closing Message & Other Companies Featured

The book closes with a letter from **Tyler Eltringham**, an Arizona State University student who founded **OneShot**, a nonprofit providing meningitis vaccines to college students while donating matching vaccines to Africa's "meningitis belt" — winning the ASU Innovation Challenge with no prior business background. Mycoskie's final message is that none of the people profiled were fully confident when they started. He urges readers not to wait for perfect readiness, reminding them that TOMS itself began as a sketch in a journal. [supersummary](https://www.supersummary.com/start-something-that-matters/summary/)

Throughout the book, Mycoskie draws inspiration from: **method** (eco-friendly cleaning), **charity: water**, **FEED Projects**, **TerraCycle**, **Zappos**, **Patagonia**, and **Chipotle**, among others. [vikoreads](https://www.vikoreads.com/post/start-something-that-matters-by-blake-mycoskie)
